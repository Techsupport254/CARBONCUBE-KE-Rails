# Master Project Governance & SDLC

Standard (MPG-002) – Front Matter

## [Executive

Summary]()

This Master Project Governance & Software Development Life Cycle
(SDLC) Standard (MPG-002) establishes a unified framework for managing
software, data, and systems engineering projects across the organization. It
provides a **consistent, repeatable methodology** for project governance and
SDLC processes to ensure projects are delivered successfully and align with the
company’s strategic objectives[[1]](https://www.opm.gov/about-us/open-government/digital-government-strategy/fitara/opm-system-development-life-cycle-policy-and-standards.pdf#:~:text=A%20key%20to%20successful%20IT,to%20the%20quality%20of%20the). In today’s complex environment, projects must meet ever-higher
expectations for  **quality, timeliness, and budget adherence** . A structured
governance framework with clear decision-making authority and defined processes
is crucial for achieving these outcomes[[2]](https://www.opm.gov/about-us/open-government/digital-government-strategy/fitara/opm-system-development-life-cycle-policy-and-standards.pdf#:~:text=technology%20investments%20within%20the%20Federal,for%20planning%2C%20managing%20and%20overseeing)[[3]](https://monday.com/blog/project-management/project-governance-your-guide-to-the-decision-making-process/#:~:text=Project%20governance%20is%20a%20management,by%20whom%20decisions%20are%20made). This document is intended for all stakeholders involved in projects –
from executives and project managers to developers and quality teams – and
outlines the standards they must follow to ensure **accountability,
transparency, and strategic alignment** in every project.

**Strategic Goals:**

- **Align Projects with Business Objectives:** Ensure every project remains
  aligned with organizational strategy and delivers intended business value[[4]](https://monday.com/blog/project-management/project-governance-your-guide-to-the-decision-making-process/#:~:text=1)[[5]](https://monday.com/blog/project-management/project-governance-your-guide-to-the-decision-making-process/#:~:text=2,management). Governance mechanisms validate that projects support strategic goals
  throughout their lifecycle.
- **Consistent & Quality Delivery:** Implement industry best practices
  through a  **consistent SDLC process** , so that projects meet customer
  expectations on time and within budget[[6]](https://www.justice.gov/archive/jmd/irm/lifecycle/exec.htm#:~:text=Systems%20Development%20Life%20Cycle%20,order%20of%20events%20for%20conducting). This standard provides a repeatable structure for planning, managing,
  and overseeing projects to achieve controlled, high-quality outcomes[[1]](https://www.opm.gov/about-us/open-government/digital-government-strategy/fitara/opm-system-development-life-cycle-policy-and-standards.pdf#:~:text=A%20key%20to%20successful%20IT,to%20the%20quality%20of%20the).
- **Clear Accountability and Oversight:** Define a hierarchy of governance
  and project roles to clarify **who makes decisions and who is responsible**
  for what. A well-designed governance structure establishes clear authority,
  reporting lines, and escalation paths, improving decision quality and
  preventing confusion[[7]](https://monday.com/blog/project-management/project-governance-your-guide-to-the-decision-making-process/#:~:text=The%20structure%20pillar%20establishes%20the,decisions%20flow%20through%20the%20organization)[[8]](https://project-management.com/understanding-responsibility-assignment-matrix-raci-matrix/#:~:text=A%20RACI%20matrix%20is%20a,role%20in%20completing%20a%20task).
- **Risk Management and Efficiency:** Apply governance controls that **manage
  risks and resource utilization** without impeding agility[[5]](https://monday.com/blog/project-management/project-governance-your-guide-to-the-decision-making-process/#:~:text=2,management)[[9]](https://monday.com/blog/project-management/project-governance-your-guide-to-the-decision-making-process/#:~:text=3). Regular oversight ensures issues are addressed early and resources
  are optimized for maximum value.
- **Compliance and Continuous Improvement:** Provide a framework that
  ensures  **compliance with organizational policies and industry regulations** ,
  and facilitates continuous process improvement. Projects are conducted in a
  controlled, measured, and documented manner with opportunities to learn and
  refine methods over time[[10]](https://www.justice.gov/archive/jmd/irm/lifecycle/exec.htm#:~:text=customer%20expectations%20when%20promised%20and,measured%2C%20documented%2C%20and%20ultimately%20improved).

In summary, this standard serves as the organization’s authoritative
guide for project governance and SDLC. By adhering to these guidelines, project
teams can increase the likelihood of delivering successful outcomes that **maximize
business value, minimize risk, and maintain quality**[[11]](https://www.opm.gov/about-us/open-government/digital-government-strategy/fitara/opm-system-development-life-cycle-policy-and-standards.pdf#:~:text=and%20projects%20over%20their%20entire,policy%20and%20standards%20guidance). All personnel (including contractors) involved in developing or
maintaining systems are expected to comply with this standard in order to
achieve consistent results across all projects[[12]](https://www.opm.gov/about-us/open-government/digital-government-strategy/fitara/opm-system-development-life-cycle-policy-and-standards.pdf#:~:text=The%20OPM%20SDLC%20applies%20to,effective%20information%20systems%20for%20OPM).

## [Purpose

and Applicability]()

**Purpose:** The purpose of MPG-002 is to **formalize
the project governance and SDLC process** for all software, data, and systems
engineering initiatives. It defines how projects should be initiated, planned,
executed, monitored, and closed in alignment with organizational strategy and
controls. By following this standard, the organization ensures that every
project **meets its requirements and supports the company’s mission and
business functions**[[13]](https://www.justice.gov/archive/jmd/irm/lifecycle/exec.htm#:~:text=Purpose%2C%20Scope%2C%20and%20Applicability). The standard acts as a mechanism to guarantee that projects are
justified, well-managed, and deliver the expected outcomes throughout their
life cycle.

**Applicability:** This standard **applies to all
projects** regardless of size, complexity, or industry domain. It is
mandatory for  **all teams and personnel** , including internal staff and
external partners, involved in software development, data engineering, systems
implementation, or technology maintenance activities[[12]](https://www.opm.gov/about-us/open-government/digital-government-strategy/fitara/opm-system-development-life-cycle-policy-and-standards.pdf#:~:text=The%20OPM%20SDLC%20applies%20to,effective%20information%20systems%20for%20OPM). Specifically, the guidelines herein must be followed by project
managers, software engineers, data engineers, business analysts, quality
assurance teams, IT operations, and any stakeholders responsible for delivering
technology solutions[[14]](https://www.justice.gov/archive/jmd/irm/lifecycle/exec.htm#:~:text=The%20primary%20audience%20for%20this,described%20throughout%20each%20life%20cycle). It covers the entire project life cycle from initial concept and
requirements gathering through design, development, testing, deployment, and
eventually system retirement. If certain projects use alternative methodologies
(e.g. agile, iterative, or commercial off-the-shelf implementations), those
teams **must still adhere to the governance principles and approval
checkpoints** defined in this standard, tailoring the detailed processes as
necessary to fit the project context[[15]](https://www.justice.gov/archive/jmd/irm/lifecycle/exec.htm#:~:text=One%20methodology%20does%20not%20fit,shelf%20%28COTS%29%20products). In essence, **all technology projects** and associated personnel
are within the scope of this SDLC governance standard, ensuring a unified
approach across the organization.

*No exceptions:* Any deviation from this
standard requires formal approval from the designated governance authority
(e.g. the Project Management Office or an executive oversight committee).
Compliance will be monitored, and non-adherence may result in project reviews
or corrective action to realign with the standard.

Every project operates within a governance hierarchy that defines  **how
decisions are made and who has authority at each level** . This hierarchy
provides a structured escalation and reporting path, ensuring that project
issues and decisions are addressed by the appropriate level of management. **Figure
1** (above placeholder) illustrates the typical governance structure for
projects in the organization, from executive oversight down to the project team
level. The key levels of this governance hierarchy are described below:

* **Executive
  Sponsor / Steering Committee:** At the top of the
  project governance hierarchy is the Executive Sponsor, often supported by
  a Project Steering Committee. The Executive Sponsor is a senior executive
  who champions the project, provides funding, and has ultimate
  decision-making authority. The Steering Committee (comprising sponsors and
  other senior stakeholders) offers strategic guidance, approves major scope
  or budget changes, and ensures the project remains aligned with
  organizational goals. This body meets periodically to review progress,
  address escalated issues, and make policy-level decisions for the project.
  They  **serve as the bridge between corporate strategy and the project** ,
  ensuring the initiative delivers value and fits within the broader
  portfolio. All major project decisions (e.g. initial charter approval,
  scope changes, go/no-go milestones) are either made or ratified at this
  level, providing top-level accountability.
* **Project Manager
  (PM):** The Project Manager is responsible for
  day-to-day project leadership and sits in the middle of the governance
  hierarchy, reporting to the Executive Sponsor/Steering Committee. The PM
  is **empowered to make operational decisions** for the project within
  the boundaries defined by the project charter and governance plan. They
  coordinate all project activities, manage the team, schedule, and budget,
  and ensure that project deliverables meet quality standards. The PM also
  serves as the primary point of communication between the project team and
  the executive governance bodies – providing status updates, raising risks
  or issues for escalation, and seeking approvals when required. The
  governance hierarchy grants the PM authority to run the project while also
  holding them accountable to higher management for compliance with this
  standard and achievement of project objectives.
* **Technical
  Leadership and Team Level:** Below the Project
  Manager, the governance structure includes technical and team leadership
  roles such as the Solution Architect, Technical Lead, or Team Leads for
  development, testing, etc. These roles have decision-making authority over
  **technical implementation details and team execution** . For example,
  the Solution/Technical Lead is responsible for design decisions, technical
  risk management, and ensuring the solution conforms to enterprise
  architecture standards. They may chair internal design reviews or change
  control boards at the project team level. While they make decisions on
  technical matters, they still operate within the scope defined by the
  Project Manager and must seek PM or sponsor approval for changes that
  affect scope, timeline, or budget. Team members (developers, engineers,
  testers, business analysts, etc.) execute project tasks according to the
  directions given by their team leads and the project plan. Issues or
  decisions that cannot be resolved at the team level are escalated up to
  the Project Manager, and if needed, further up to the Steering Committee
  or Sponsor, following the hierarchy.

This governance hierarchy ensures  **clarity of authority and
escalation** : routine decisions are handled at the project team level for
efficiency, while high-impact decisions are elevated to senior management for
oversight[[7]](https://monday.com/blog/project-management/project-governance-your-guide-to-the-decision-making-process/#:~:text=The%20structure%20pillar%20establishes%20the,decisions%20flow%20through%20the%20organization). By clearly defining these layers, the organization prevents both
micromanagement from above and ungoverned decision-making below. Everyone
involved in the project knows where to turn for approvals and how information
flows upward and downward in the decision chain. This structure not only speeds
up decision-making (by addressing issues at the right level) but also provides
accountability, as each tier has defined responsibilities for project outcomes.

## [Roles

& RACI Matrix]()

Effective project governance relies on clearly defined  **roles and
responsibilities** . This section outlines the key project roles and provides
a RACI matrix (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) to clarify each
role’s involvement in major project activities. Defining roles and using a RACI
model helps prevent confusion and overlapping duties by making sure everyone
understands “who does what” in the project[[8]](https://project-management.com/understanding-responsibility-assignment-matrix-raci-matrix/#:~:text=A%20RACI%20matrix%20is%20a,role%20in%20completing%20a%20task).

### [Key Project Roles and

Responsibilities]()

·
**Executive Sponsor:** A senior executive who  **owns the project’s business outcome** . The
Sponsor provides high-level direction, secures funding, and champions the
project’s strategic importance. They are ultimately **Accountable (A)** for
the project’s success and authorizes key decisions and resources. The Sponsor
resolves escalated issues and ensures the project’s goals align with
organizational strategy.

·
**Steering Committee:** A group of senior stakeholders and domain experts (which often
includes the Sponsor) that provides  **governance oversight** . The committee
reviews project status, risks, and changes. It makes critical decisions on
scope or priority and ensures cross-departmental impacts are managed. Members
of this committee are typically **Consulted (C)** or **Informed (I)** on
major tasks, and collectively **Accountable** for governance decisions.

·
**Project Manager (PM):** The PM is **Responsible (R)** for planning and executing the
project. They manage the schedule, budget, scope, and team. The PM coordinates
communication among stakeholders and ensures project deliverables meet
requirements. They are the **day-to-day decision-maker** for operational
issues and are **Accountable** for meeting project objectives within the
constraints set by the Sponsor. The PM also keeps the Sponsor and Steering
Committee **Informed** through regular status reports and seeks their
guidance or approval on major issues.

·
**Business Analyst (BA) / Product
Owner:** This role serves as the liaison between
business stakeholders and the technical team. The BA is **Responsible** for
eliciting requirements, documenting specifications, and ensuring the solution
meets business needs. They often facilitate discussions to clarify business
processes and define acceptance criteria. In agile contexts, a Product Owner
would prioritize the backlog. The BA/Product Owner is **Consulted** on
design and scope decisions (since their input on requirements is crucial), and
they keep stakeholders **Informed** about requirement changes.

·
**Solution Architect / Technical
Lead:** The Technical Lead (or Architect) is **Responsible**
for the overall technical solution and design decisions. They establish the
architecture, choose frameworks or platforms, and ensure adherence to technical
standards. This role provides technical guidance to developers and resolves
complex technical issues. The Technical Lead is **Accountable** for the
quality of the technical deliverables and that the design fulfills the
requirements and aligns with enterprise architecture. They may also be **Consulted**
by the PM or Sponsor on feasibility and by the team on implementation details.

·
**Development Team:** Comprising software developers, data engineers, and other
implementation specialists, the development team is **Responsible** for
building the product according to the requirements and design. They write code,
configure systems, and integrate components. Team members may have sub-role
specializations (frontend, backend, database, etc.), but collectively they own
the execution of development tasks. They are **Consulted** by the Technical
Lead on technical approaches and keep the PM **Informed** of progress or
issues.

·
**Quality Assurance (QA)
Lead/Team:** The QA role is **Responsible** for
testing the product to verify it meets quality standards and requirements. QA
develops test plans, executes test cases (functional tests, performance tests,
etc.), and reports defects. The QA Lead ensures that identified issues are
addressed by the development team. They are **Accountable** for the quality
assurance process and sign-off on the product’s quality. QA is **Consulted**
during requirements and design phases to ensure testability, and they keep the
PM and Sponsor **Informed** of any major quality risks (for example, if
critical defects are found).

·
**DevOps / Release Engineer:** (If applicable) This role is **Responsible** for build automation,
continuous integration, and managing deployment pipelines. They create and
maintain the infrastructure as code, deployment scripts, and environment
configurations (often using tools like Docker and CI/CD platforms). The DevOps
engineer ensures that the software can be reliably and repeatedly built,
tested, and deployed across environments. They are **Consulted** on matters
of environment setup, deployment scheduling, and performance monitoring, and
keep the team **Informed** about deployment status or infrastructure
changes.

Each
project may have additional roles (e.g. UX Designer, Data Scientist, Security
Specialist) depending on its nature, but the above are the core roles common to
most projects. **Roles and responsibilities must be documented** at the
start of the project so every team member and stakeholder understands their
duties and decision-making authority.

## [Naming

Conventions]()

Standardizing naming conventions for files, code, and documentation is
essential for maintaining an organized and professional project environment.
Consistent naming makes it easier for team members to understand the project
artifacts and for new members to get up to speed[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naming_convention_(programming)#:~:text=,than%20syntax%20and%20style%20preferences). This section defines the naming standards that all projects must
follow. Adhering to these conventions reduces ambiguity and errors, improves
readability, and enables automated tools to more easily parse and manage
project files[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naming_convention_(programming)#:~:text=Reasons%20for%20using%20a%20naming,include%20the%20following)[[23]](https://www.itglue.com/blog/naming-conventions-examples-formats-best-practices/#:~:text=The%20main%20purpose%20of%20naming,files%2C%20resource%20wastage%2C%20errors%2C%20etc).

**General Principles:**

- **Clarity and Descriptiveness:** Names should be self-explanatory and
  describe the content or purpose. Avoid acronyms or abbreviations that are not
  widely understood. For example, a requirements document might be named ProjectX_Requirements_v1.0.docx instead of just reqs.docx.
- **Consistency:** Use the same naming patterns across the project for
  similar items. Once a convention is chosen (for instance, using camelCase for
  variable names or kebab-case for file names), apply it uniformly throughout the
  project[[24]](https://achermouk.com/write-better-names-for-your-variables-functions-and-classes/#:~:text=SIMO%20achermouk,Avoid). Consistency helps developers and stakeholders quickly recognize what
  a file or identifier represents.
- **Avoid Special Characters and Spaces:** File names should use only
  alphanumeric characters and allowable symbols (e.g., hyphen or underscore). **Do
  not use spaces or characters like ~, &, %** in file or directory names,
  as these can cause issues in various operating systems and tools[[25]](https://www.itglue.com/blog/naming-conventions-examples-formats-best-practices/#:~:text=Alphabetic%20%26%20numerical%20characters%20only). Use a hyphen - or underscore _ instead of spaces to separate words in file names.
- **Use of Dates and Versioning:** When applicable, include version numbers
  or dates in filenames to distinguish revisions. Dates should follow a standard
  format (preferably YYYYMMDD for chronological sorting)[[26]](https://www.itglue.com/blog/naming-conventions-examples-formats-best-practices/#:~:text=Establish%20a%20standard%20date%20format). For example, DesignSpec_20251103_v2.docx clearly indicates the date (2025-11-03) and version 2. Version control
  systems (like Git) will track changes in code, but for documents and
  deliverables, use a version suffix to avoid confusion between drafts[[27]](https://www.itglue.com/blog/naming-conventions-examples-formats-best-practices/#:~:text=Specify%20a%20version).

**Code Naming Standards:**

In source code, follow conventional identifier naming schemes to improve code
readability and maintainability. The specific style may vary by programming
language (for instance, Python prefers snake_case for variables, while Java
uses camelCase), but as a baseline the following conventions are recommended:

* **camelCase:** Start with a lowercase letter and capitalize the first letter of
  each subsequent word, with no spaces or symbols. Commonly used for
  variable names, function names, and object properties in many languages[[28]](https://www.freecodecamp.org/news/programming-naming-conventions-explained/#:~:text=What%20is%20Camel%20Case%3F).

  *Example:* customerName, calculateTotal() – here “customerName” has a lowercase c and then capital N for the second word.
* **PascalCase:** Every word starts with an uppercase letter (no spaces or
  separators). Typically used for class names, type names, or filenames in
  some cases[[29]](https://www.freecodecamp.org/news/programming-naming-conventions-explained/#:~:text=What%20is%20Pascal%20Case%3F).

  *Example:* CustomerAccount, OrderProcessor – both words “Customer” and “Account” are capitalized in CustomerAccount.
* **snake_case:** All letters are lowercase, words are separated by underscores _. This style is often used in Python and some database naming.
  It’s also suitable for filenames or config keys where space needs to be
  represented clearly[[30]](https://www.freecodecamp.org/news/programming-naming-conventions-explained/#:~:text=What%20is%20Snake%20Case%3F).

  *Example:* customer_account or process_order.py – words are lowercased and separated by underscores.
* **kebab-case:** Similar to snake_case but uses hyphens - to separate words (no spaces). This is frequently seen in
  filenames, URLs, or CSS class names, but not in most programming language
  variables (because hyphen might be an illegal character in code
  identifiers)[[31]](https://www.freecodecamp.org/news/programming-naming-conventions-explained/#:~:text=What%20is%20Kebab%20Case%3F). Use kebab-case for
  file and folder names if preferred for readability.

  *Example:* user-data.csv, error-log.txt – multi-word names are in lowercase with a hyphen between words.
* **UPPER_SNAKE_CASE:** Constants (values meant to not change) should be named with all
  letters uppercase, using underscores to separate words. Many languages and
  style guides (like Python’s PEP8 or Java’s constant conventions) follow
  this for constant values.

  *Example:* MAX_RETRY_COUNT, DEFAULT_TIMEOUT_SECONDS.

These naming conventions make the role of each identifier immediately
clear (for instance, seeing a PascalCase name suggests it’s a type or class,
whereas seeing lowerCamelCase suggests a variable or function). Consistency
here helps in code reviews and when navigating the codebase[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naming_convention_(programming)#:~:text=,than%20syntax%20and%20style%20preferences). Each project or development team may maintain a more detailed style
guide, but it must  **not conflict with the above conventions** .

**File and Document Naming Standards:**

All project documentation and files (requirements documents, design specs, test
plans, etc.) should follow a structured naming scheme:

·
Include the **project identifier
or acronym** at the start of file names to easily associate files with the
project. For example, if the project is “Alpha”, a design document could be Alpha_DesignSpec_v1.0.docx.

·
Include a brief **description of
the content** (e.g., DesignSpec, TestPlan, UserGuide). Use capital letters to
start each main word or use CamelCase/PascalCase for the descriptive part to
improve readability (as seen in the example above)[[32]](https://www.itglue.com/blog/naming-conventions-examples-formats-best-practices/#:~:text=).

·
If multiple versions or drafts are
created, append a **version number** (v1.0, v2.0 etc.) or date stamp. This helps team members identify the latest
version at a glance[[27]](https://www.itglue.com/blog/naming-conventions-examples-formats-best-practices/#:~:text=Specify%20a%20version). Avoid relying solely on vague terms like “final” or “updated” in
filenames – use incremental versioning.

·
**Folders:** Organize files into directories by category (e.g., /Requirements, /Design, /Test, /UserDocs). Use clear folder names and apply similar conventions (perhaps
PascalCase or all-lowercase) consistently for directory names. For instance,
have a folder ProjectAlpha_TestResults_2025 for test output files of project Alpha in year 2025.

·
**Examples:** A requirements document for Project Alpha might be named Alpha_Requirements_v1.2.docx; a source code file might be UserController.java (class in PascalCase); a configuration file might be app_config.yaml (snake_case) or app-config.yaml
(kebab-case) depending on the adopted style.

By enforcing these naming standards, the project ensures that anyone
can quickly locate and understand files and code entities. For example, a
developer can infer from a filename like *InventoryService_TestPlan_v20251103.docx*
that it’s a test plan for the InventoryService project dated Nov 3, 2025.
Moreover, consistent naming prevents errors such as duplicate files or
misidentification, and facilitates smoother handovers and maintenance since the
naming conveys meaningful information about each item[[33]](https://www.itglue.com/blog/naming-conventions-examples-formats-best-practices/#:~:text=What%20are%20the%20benefits%20of,naming%20conventions)[[34]](https://www.itglue.com/blog/naming-conventions-examples-formats-best-practices/#:~:text=,versions%20of%20already%20existing%20files).

## [Engineering Toolchain Baseline]()

To support a robust SDLC, the organization mandates a baseline set of
engineering tools and practices that every project must utilize. This ensures
that all teams have a common infrastructure for version control, build and
deployment, and environment management, which in turn improves collaboration
and efficiency. Adopting standard tools (like Git and Docker) and processes
(CI/CD) across projects also helps in maintaining quality and security, as well
as onboarding team members who can expect a familiar toolchain in any project.

**1. Version Control – Git:** All source code,
scripts, and configuration files **must be stored in a Git repository** (the
company may host Git centrally via platforms like GitHub, GitLab, or
Bitbucket). Git is the most widely used version control system in the industry[[35]](https://medium.com/@Adekola_Olawale/part-4-mastering-git-a52f389accbb#:~:text=In%20this%20article%2C%20we%20will,crucial%20role%20in%20DevOps%20workflows) and is crucial for enabling collaborative development and DevOps
workflows. Every project team shall use Git to track changes, allowing for
branching, pull requests (for code review), and history tracking of all
modifications. Best practices include committing changes with clear messages,
using feature branches for development, and merging via reviewed pull requests
to the main branch. Version control not only provides an audit trail of who
changed what and when, but also facilitates continuous integration by
triggering automated builds/tests on each commit. In summary, **no code should
exist outside of version control** – this standard ensures traceability and
the ability to roll back if needed.

**2. Containerization – Docker:** The organization
standardizes on Docker for creating consistent runtime environments across
development, testing, and production. Docker allows packaging an application
with all its dependencies into a lightweight container, ensuring that it will
run the same way in any environment[[36]](https://medium.com/techtrends-digest/why-docker-is-the-future-of-devops-3be23969e155#:~:text=Docker%20is%20an%20open,management%20of%20applications%20using%20containers)[[37]](https://medium.com/techtrends-digest/why-docker-is-the-future-of-devops-3be23969e155#:~:text=At%20its%20core%2C%20Docker%20helps,regardless%20of%20where%20it%E2%80%99s%20deployed). All projects deploying applications to server environments (including
virtual private servers or cloud instances) should containerize their
applications using Docker. This practice eliminates the “works on my machine”
problem by providing a uniform environment for the software[[38]](https://medium.com/techtrends-digest/why-docker-is-the-future-of-devops-3be23969e155#:~:text=This%20consistency%20eliminates%20the%20%E2%80%9Cit,a%20testing%20or%20production%20environment). For instance, if a web service is developed, a Docker image should be
made that includes the web service and its required runtime (Java, Node.js,
Python, etc.), so that the same image can be used for testing and deployment.
The standard requires maintaining **Dockerfiles** in the project repository
to build these images, and using a registry (like Docker Hub or an internal
registry) to store and version-control the container images. By using Docker,
teams also simplify dependency management and can leverage container
orchestration in higher environments if needed. *(Note: In cases where Docker
is not applicable, such as certain data engineering or embedded systems
projects, an equivalent mechanism for environment consistency must be used.)*

**3. Continuous Integration/Continuous Delivery (CI/CD):** Every project must incorporate **automated build and test processes**
as part of a CI pipeline. Upon code check-in to Git (or at least for each merge
to the main branch), an automated build should compile the code, run unit
tests, and package the application. Tools such as Jenkins, GitLab CI, GitHub
Actions, or Azure DevOps Pipelines can be used to orchestrate these steps. The
CI system should also build Docker images if applicable, and run automated
tests within those containers to verify that the application works in a
production-like environment[[39]](https://medium.com/techtrends-digest/why-docker-is-the-future-of-devops-3be23969e155#:~:text=Docker%20enables%20DevOps%20teams%20to,deploy%20applications%20in%20identical%20environments)[[40]](https://medium.com/techtrends-digest/why-docker-is-the-future-of-devops-3be23969e155#:~:text=Using%20Docker%20in%20a%20CI%2FCD,Hub%20or%20a%20private%20registry). Continuous Delivery means that the pipeline can deploy the
application to a staging or production environment in an automated fashion once
tests pass and required approvals are in place. Basic CI/CD considerations
mandated by this standard include:

·
**Automated Testing:** Include unit tests in the codebase and aim for a meaningful level of
test coverage. The CI pipeline must execute these tests on each build to catch
regressions early. Optionally, integration tests and static code analysis
(linting, security scans) should be part of the pipeline to maintain code
quality.

·
**Build Artifacts:** The pipeline should produce packaged artifacts (such as compiled
binaries, Docker images, or library packages) that can be deployed. These
artifacts should be versioned (matching the release version) and stored in an
artifact repository or container registry.

·
**Environment Parity:** The CI/CD process should promote the same artifact through multiple
environments (Dev → Test → Prod) to ensure what was tested is exactly what is
released. Docker greatly facilitates this by encapsulating the environment.
Configuration for different environments (like database strings, API endpoints)
can be managed via external config files or environment variables, but the code
and binary remain the same through the pipeline.

·
**Deployment Automation:** Scripts or tools for deployment must be used instead of manual
procedures. Whether it’s infrastructure-as-code (for provisioning
servers/containers) or simple deployment scripts, they should be part of the
project repository. For example, a Jenkins pipeline might automatically deploy
a new Docker container to a test server when a build passes, or use Kubernetes
to update a service. Having automated deployment reduces human error and speeds
up release cycles[[40]](https://medium.com/techtrends-digest/why-docker-is-the-future-of-devops-3be23969e155#:~:text=Using%20Docker%20in%20a%20CI%2FCD,Hub%20or%20a%20private%20registry). Before deploying to production, the pipeline can pause for a manual
approval (often by the Project Manager or Product Owner), which is a governance
checkpoint in line with the change management procedures.

By establishing this common toolchain, the organization ensures that
all projects benefit from modern development practices:  **source control,
reproducible environments, and automation** . This not only improves
collaboration and efficiency (developers can share code easily, and ops teams
get consistent deploys) but also enforces a level of quality control (since
builds and tests are run uniformly). Projects that adopt this toolchain have a
significantly higher chance of avoiding integration problems and deployment
failures, as evidenced by industry’s DevOps outcomes[[38]](https://medium.com/techtrends-digest/why-docker-is-the-future-of-devops-3be23969e155#:~:text=This%20consistency%20eliminates%20the%20%E2%80%9Cit,a%20testing%20or%20production%20environment). Moreover, using widely adopted tools like Git and Docker means teams
leverage community knowledge and do not reinvent the wheel for common tasks.
New projects should begin by setting up their Git repo, CI pipeline, and Docker
environment configurations per this standard. The Engineering Toolchain
Baseline will be periodically reviewed and updated by the Engineering
Governance Board to incorporate new tools or improve practices, but **compliance
with the current baseline is mandatory** for all ongoing projects.
